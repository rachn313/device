LUMEE

Designed for people who work from home, our device enables users to remotely signal when they are in a meeting to other members of their household.

Designers: Lily Chu, Rachel Navarette, Rebecca Arango

Problem Statement

Our goal is to create a tangible user interface that helps people study and work at home.

In multi-person households, it is difficult to communicate or know when others are in a meeting and need space and quiet.

Lumee is a TUI that allows family members or people living together in one household to remotely signal to each other that they are currently in a virtual meeting. They are also able to designate the importance of a meeting, ranging from high priority to low priority.

No more memorizing other people's schedules or accidentally zoom bombing in the background. Lumee's got your back!

Conceptual Design

Users communicate with each other via lit and unlit LEDs. Ultimately we imagined each user of a household to have their own Lumee device, a small box with strips of LEDs on top. One strip would be a row of three LEDs and each strip is a different color. Each user would be assigned to a different strip, and by extension a different color.


On the side of each device are three buttons. If the user presses the first button, the first LED in their assigned strip will light up on each Lumee device. One lit lED indicates that this user is in a low-priority meeting. If the user presses the second button, the second LED will light up on each device. Two lit LEDS indicate that this user is in a medium-priority meeting. If the user presses the third button, the third LED will light up on each device. Three lit LEDs indicate that this user is in a high-priority meeting.

Design Process

Initial Design

Intially we imagined that the LEDs could be controlled with an on/off button and a slider to adjust light intensity. Below, we outline a few initial artifacts that helped us communicate our initial design: user scenario, storyboard, visual and tangible design, and a low-fidelity prototype.


Initial User Scenario

"The Smith family has two parents working from home (the father is a business consultant, and the mother is a middle school teacher), a child in high school (in a hybrid learning environment), a child in college (located in different timezone) studying remotely from home, and a child who is in elementary school."
"Each of the five family members are constantly attending meetings or classes at various times of the day, and often find themselves struggling to find privacy, or being able to communicate to one another that they should not be interrupted. Mrs. Smith teaches classes all day and is frequently interrupted by her husband and children, asking for food, playtime, or other things. At 9:00 PM, one of her students located in a different time zone requests an impromptu 1:1 meeting. This is a problematic time for Mrs. Smith, because this is when she usually puts her youngest to bed and checks on her other children. Mrs. Smith has just finished a meeting with another student, so she does not have time to run through the house between the meetings to let her husband know that he will have to take over.""


Initial Storyboard

One of the most crucial features of the system is the capability of the LEDs to turn on and off on each device in the household. This network of connected devices and the dynamic LEDs allow for understandable and correct communication between users. If one user is assigned to the red LED, and they turn their LED on, the red LED on every other user’s device should turn on. If the red user adjusts their LED, then the brightness of the every red LED on all of the devices should be adjusted accordingly. The simple and rapid on and off switch on a light captures the nature of the modern workplace: fast-paced and ever-changing, making it the perfect representation for a state of work. Additionally, the portable size and connected network allows each user to have their device in constant proximity to them, wherever they may be.


Initial Visual and Tangible Design

We purposefully designed simple features for this device, to ensure that users would intuitively understand how to correctly use the device to their advantage. We envisioned a box with a cluster of LEDs on its top. Each LED would represent a different user, so each user could be assigned and identifiable with a unique color. Two buttons and a slider, located on the side of the box, could be used to turn the device on, to turn the user’s LED on, and to adjust the intensity of the light, respectively. In order to allow for a clean design, the electronic equipment, such as the motherboard and battery pack, would be hidden inside the box. In addition to focusing on a simple design, we also wanted the entire device to be comfortable on a flat surface, so that it could be stored near the user in their workspace, likely on a desk or table.


Initial Low-Fidelity Prototype

This low-fidelity prototype was constructed using paper, tape, pipe cleaners, beads, sequins, and mini pom-poms. Ultimately, this prototype captures not only the look and feel of our device, but also provides a limited simulation of potential user interaction: using the slider. If this were a high-fidelity prototype, the slider would adjust the intensity of a specific LED.


Final Design

Although these initial artifacts outline an intensity-based light communication system, we ultimately scratched that idea. After recieving feedback from our classmates, we realized that our design could have more user-friendly attributes.
We made three changes to the initial design: accumulation (three LEDS instead of one), buttons to control each LED, and less users per device.
For more information, visit our Concept Design (linked on the hompage)!


Functional Prototype

This is our final functional prototype, which looks a little different from our final conceptual design. We found that in order to implement functional prototype using the technological materials available to us, we needed to adjust our final aesthetic design. The lights located next to the set of buttons are the LEDs assigned to the device owner. The lights perpendicular of those are LEDs assigned to other users.

Technical Implementation

The technical implementation of our functional prototype consists of two components: hardware and software.

The majority of our hardware was chosen in order to work with the motherboard we were using, an Adafruit Flora. We used Adafruit Neopixels, which are programmable LEDs, Adafruit On/Off Buttons, and conductive thread to create the final circuit. We ultimately designed two circuits, one for the LEDs dedicated to the device owner, and one dedicated to another user. The owner's LEDs are constructed with three separate series circuits each consisting of a Neopixel and an On/Off Button, connected to PIN 6 on the Adafruit Flora. The other user's set of three LEDs are connect in a parallel circuit.

Our software consisted of a sketch written in the Arduino IDE, which programs the set of LEDs to turn on and off accordingly. We used a compatable Adafruit Library to write much of our language. To see our full code, visit our Github Repository, linked on the main page.

Previous Prototypes

Although for the final product, we used three series circuits to construct the device owner's LEDs and on/off buttons, we explored a parallel circuit at first. We initially had hoped to build out a parallel circuit for both rows of LEDs, but continued to run into problems. Eventually, we figured out that the metallic thread was too unstable to support such a complex parallel circuit, so we redesigned our circuitry into a set of three series circuits.
